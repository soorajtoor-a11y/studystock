FBLA STUDY CONTENT — INTRODUCTION TO FBLA (2025–2026)
LONG-FORM EDITION
Compiled from fbla.org, official competitive events guidelines, state
chapter handbooks, and commonly tested facts from practice materials.
Test = 100 multiple-choice questions, 50 minutes. 9th-10th grade only.
============================================================

CONSTRUCTED KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES
(FBLA publishes knowledge areas but no objective list for this event;
this path is modeled on the official format.)

A. Foundational Documents
1. Recite the mission, pledge, creed, goals, and code of ethics (exact wording).
2. Identify which document a quoted line comes from.
B. History
1. Sequence the major events in FBLA history, 1937-present.
2. Identify the founder, first chapter, and key leaders.
C. Structure and Governance

D. Programs, Conferences, and Competitive Events

PART 1 — FOUNDATIONAL DOCUMENTS (test favorites; drill VERBATIM)

1.1 MISSION STATEMENT
"FBLA inspires and prepares students to become community-minded
business leaders in a global society through relevant career
preparation and leadership experiences."
Key phrases the test blanks out: "community-minded business
leaders," "global society," "relevant career preparation,"
"leadership experiences."

1.2 PLEDGE
"I solemnly promise to support the mission of Future Business
Leaders of America, abide by its Code of Ethics and Code of Conduct,
and develop the qualities necessary to become a community-minded
business leader."

1.3 CREED — "I believe:" (seven beliefs)
1. "Education is the right of every person."
2. "The future depends on mutual understanding and cooperation among
   business, industry, labor, religious, family, and educational
   institutions, as well as people around the world. I agree to do
   my utmost to bring about understanding and cooperation among all
   of these groups."
3. "Every person should prepare for a useful occupation and carry on
   that occupation in a manner that brings the greatest good to the
   greatest number."
4. "Every person should actively work toward improving social,
   political, community, and family life."
5. "Every person has the right to earn a living at a useful
   occupation."
6. "Every person should take responsibility for carrying out
   assigned tasks in a manner that brings credit to self,
   associates, school, and community."
7. "I have the responsibility to work efficiently and to think
   clearly. I promise to use my abilities to make the world a better
   place for everyone."
Frequently tested: which institution list appears in belief #2
(business, industry, labor, religious, family, educational);
"greatest good to the greatest number"; the four life areas in #4
(social, political, community, family).

1.4 GOALS (nine — know all, in substance)
1. Develop competent, aggressive business leadership.
2. Strengthen the confidence of students in themselves and their work.
3. Create more interest in and understanding of American business
   enterprise.
4. Encourage members in the development of individual projects that
   contribute to the improvement of home, business, and community.
5. Develop character, prepare for useful citizenship, and foster
   patriotism.
6. Encourage and practice efficient money management.
7. Encourage scholarship and promote school loyalty.
8. Assist students in the establishment of occupational goals.
9. Facilitate the transition from school to work.
Watch for trick options: "competent, AGGRESSIVE business leadership"
(not "assertive"); "foster patriotism" appears with character and
citizenship.

1.5 CODE OF ETHICS — "I commit to:" (eight commitments)
1. Collaborating with a commitment to inclusivity, diversity,
   equity, care, and respect for the dignity and worth of every
   individual.
2. Undertaking each task with a clear understanding of my
   responsibilities, aiming to execute all work to the highest
   standard.
3. Performing all of my actions and duties ethically with the utmost
   honesty, accountability, and transparency.
4. Establishing and nurturing relationships based on trust.
5. Being receptive to constructive feedback and seeking
   opportunities for personal and professional growth.
6. Complying with both the specific guidelines and the overarching
   principles of my school's policies and FBLA's standards.
7. Promoting a culture of respect through my dress, behavior, and
   communication, positively representing myself, school, and future
   profession.
8. Contributing to the betterment of my local and global community
   by engaging in projects and initiatives that bring the greatest
   good to the greatest number.

PART 2 — HISTORY (timeline questions are guaranteed)

- 1937: Hamden L. Forkner Sr., professor at Columbia University
  (Teachers College) and creator of the Forkner shorthand system,
  proposes ONE national organization to unite the many local
  business clubs in high schools across the nation.
- 1940: The name "Future Business Leaders of America" is chosen;
  FBLA is established. Early sponsorship came through the National
  Council for Business Education (later part of the National
  Business Education Association, NBEA).
- FEBRUARY 3, 1942: The FIRST FBLA CHAPTER is chartered at
  SCIENCE HILL HIGH SCHOOL in JOHNSON CITY, TENNESSEE.
  (First chapter facts are the single most-tested history item.)
- 1958: PHI BETA LAMBDA (PBL) — the postsecondary/college division —
  is created; first PBL chapter at the University of Northern Iowa.
- 1969: FBLA is granted INDEPENDENT status as a nonprofit
  educational student association (separates from NBEA sponsorship;
  incorporated as FBLA-PBL, Inc.).
- 1973: Edward D. Miller becomes the first full-time Executive
  Director.
- 1979: The Alumni Division is founded (later the Professional
  Division, now FBLA Network).
- 1981: The Conrad N. Hilton Foundation donates 1.6 acres of land in
  Reston, Virginia, for a National Center.
- 1987: National membership surpasses 200,000.
- 1991: The FBLA NATIONAL CENTER opens in RESTON, VIRGINIA
  (still the headquarters).
- 1994: FBLA-MIDDLE LEVEL is formed for students in grades 5-9.
- 1997: Jean Buckley appointed President and CEO.
- 2019: Alexander T. Graham appointed President and CEO.
- 2022: Major rebrand — new logo; divisions renamed FBLA High
  School, FBLA Middle School, FBLA Collegiate (Phi Beta Lambda
  becomes FBLA Collegiate), and FBLA Network.

PART 3 — STRUCTURE AND GOVERNANCE

3.1 The four divisions
1. FBLA Middle School (grades 5-9; formed 1994 as FBLA-ML)
2. FBLA High School (grades 9-12; the largest division, ~200,000+
   members — FBLA is the largest business CTSO in the world)
3. FBLA Collegiate (formerly Phi Beta Lambda; college students)
4. FBLA Network (professionals, alumni, and supporters)

3.2 Levels of organization (bottom to top)
LOCAL CHAPTER (school-based; usually tied to the business education
department; adviser is typically a business education teacher; each
chapter has its own officer team) -> DISTRICT/REGION/SECTION within
the state (varies by state) -> STATE CHAPTER (requires at least 5
active local chapters; has its own state officer team — commonly
president, VP, secretary, treasurer, parliamentarian, sometimes
historian/reporter/webmaster) -> NATIONAL.

3.3 National regions (High School & Middle School) — FIVE:
Eastern, Southern, North Central, Mountain Plains, Western.
International chapters belong to the Eastern Region.

3.4 National officers (FBLA High School) — NINE:
President, Secretary, Treasurer, Parliamentarian, and five Vice
Presidents (one per region). Elected by VOTING DELEGATES at the
National Leadership Conference; installed at the Awards of
Excellence Program; serve ONE-YEAR terms; responsible for the
annual program of work. (FBLA Collegiate elects its own officers;
the HS officers also represent Middle School.)

3.5 Governance
Board of Directors: the CEO, business leaders, state educators,
business education teachers, and the division national presidents.
A professional staff at the National Center (Reston, VA) runs
day-to-day operations, led by the President & CEO.

PART 4 — PROGRAMS, CONFERENCES, COMPETITIVE EVENTS

4.1 The conference ladder
Regional/District conferences -> STATE LEADERSHIP CONFERENCE (SLC)
-> NATIONAL LEADERSHIP CONFERENCE (NLC, each summer). Placing at
states (state entry limit: up to 4 entries per event) qualifies
members for nationals. Other national events: NATIONAL FALL
LEADERSHIP CONFERENCE (NFLC — motivation/leadership, no
competitions), and virtual/regional leadership programs.

4.2 Competitive event categories (High School)
1. OBJECTIVE TESTS (individual; 100 MC questions, 50 minutes —
   this event is one)
2. ROLE PLAY events (team; case + interactive performance)
3. PRESENTATION events (prepared presentations/projects)
4. PRODUCTION events (produce documents/files under test conditions)
5. CHAPTER events (whole-chapter projects: Community Service
   Project, Local Chapter Annual Business Report)
Key rules: one individual/team event + one chapter event per member
at NLC; top-10 NLC finishers can't repeat that event; max 10 winners
recognized per event; 5 penalty points for dress code violations or
late arrival; competitors must know the Competitive Events Policy &
Procedures Manual, HONOR CODE, CODE OF CONDUCT, and DRESS CODE.

4.3 Recognition and education programs
- BUSINESS ACHIEVEMENT AWARDS (BAA): individual, self-paced business
  and leadership program completed through FBLA Connect;
  historically four levels — FUTURE, BUSINESS, LEADER, AMERICA
  (America level = highest, recognized at NLC). Verify current
  level names in FBLA Connect, as the rebrand updated program
  structures.
- Community Service Awards (CSA): recognizes cumulative service
  hours (historically 50 / 200 / 500 hour tiers).
- Scholarships, skills certifications, career portfolio programs,
  and member discounts.
- FBLA Connect: the national membership/program platform.

4.4 Calendar items every member should know
- Dues deadline for NLC eligibility: MARCH 1, 11:59 p.m. Eastern.
- FBLA WEEK: the second week of FEBRUARY (includes themed days,
  e.g., Adviser Appreciation Day on Wednesday).
- AMERICAN ENTERPRISE DAY: NOVEMBER 15 (chapters run projects
  promoting free enterprise).
- NLC: summer; NFLC: fall.

PART 5 — POLICIES (dress code, conduct)

5.1 Dress code (business professional expectations)
- Purpose: mirror the expectations of the modern corporate
  workplace; applies to ALL attendees at official FBLA functions —
  competitors, voting delegates, officer candidates, and members.
- Professional attire examples: business suits, blazers/sport
  coats, dress slacks, collared dress shirts, ties, blouses,
  skirts/dresses of professional length, dress shoes.
- Not acceptable (typical list): jeans, athletic wear, shorts,
  t-shirts, flip-flops/athletic shoes, hats.
- Enforcement at competition: 5 penalty points for violations; may
  be denied participation for noncompliance.

5.2 Code of Conduct & Honor Code (competition context)
- Members represent their school, state, and FBLA at all times;
  conduct violations can disqualify a competitor.
- Honor Code: complete all work honestly; no unauthorized aid on
  tests; electronic devices off and stored during events — a
  visible device is treated as an Honor Code violation.

PART 6 — IDENTITY, PUBLICATIONS, PARTNERS (classic trivia section)

- EMBLEM/CREST WORDS: "SERVICE, EDUCATION, PROGRESS" — memorize;
  a favorite trick question adds a fourth word (leadership,
  innovation) that does NOT belong.
- OFFICIAL COLORS: BLUE and GOLD.
- NATIONAL PUBLICATION: "Tomorrow's Business Leader" (member
  magazine; now digital via fbla.org newsroom).
- NATIONAL SERVICE PARTNER: the ALZHEIMER'S ASSOCIATION (current);
  the longtime previous partner was the MARCH OF DIMES (decades of
  partnership; March for Babies fundraising).
- MEMBERSHIP PLATFORM: FBLA Connect. WEBSITE: fbla.org.
- FBLA is a CAREER AND TECHNICAL STUDENT ORGANIZATION (CTSO) — one
  of the CTSOs recognized by the U.S. Department of Education; the
  largest business CTSO in the world; 250,000+ total members across
  all divisions; largest state memberships historically include
  Georgia and Missouri.
