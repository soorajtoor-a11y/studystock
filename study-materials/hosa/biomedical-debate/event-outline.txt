HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — BIOMEDICAL DEBATE (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA Competitive Event Guidelines 2025-2026 (Round 1 written-test knowledge domain).
Format: Round 1 Written Knowledge Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (qualifies for the team/performance round).
Item counts per content area are proportional estimates by knowledge-area weight —
confirm against the current guideline, in parentheses.
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Knowledge of debate structure and argumentation, evidence evaluation, and
core biomedical ethics issues (genetic engineering, end-of-life care,
resource allocation, research ethics, healthcare policy) that underpin the
current year's debate topic and general biomedical/health-policy debate.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Debate Structure & Formats (6 items)
1. Identify the standard structure of a debate round (constructive, rebuttal, cross-examination, closing).
2. Differentiate the affirmative/proposition side's burden from the negative/opposition side's burden.
3. Explain the purpose of a resolution/topic statement in framing a debate.
4. Explain the role of time limits and speaking order in a structured debate format.
5. Differentiate a policy debate (should we act?) from a value debate (is this ethical/desirable?).
6. Explain the purpose of a case/constructive speech in establishing a team's position.

B. Argumentation & Logic (6 items)
1. Identify the components of a well-formed argument (claim, evidence/data, warrant/reasoning).
2. Differentiate deductive from inductive reasoning in constructing an argument.
3. Identify common logical fallacies (ad hominem, slippery slope, straw man, false dilemma, appeal to emotion).
4. Explain the concept of burden of proof and who holds it in a given debate.
5. Explain how to weigh competing arguments (impact, probability, magnitude, timeframe).
6. Explain the difference between a link (connection between argument steps) and an impact (why it matters).

C. Evidence Evaluation & Research Literacy (7 items)
1. Differentiate primary from secondary sources in biomedical research.
2. Explain criteria for evaluating source credibility (peer review, authorship, recency, conflict of interest).
3. Differentiate correlation from causation when evaluating a research claim.
4. Explain the significance of study sample size and methodology in evaluating evidence strength.
5. Identify signs of biased or unreliable evidence (cherry-picking, outdated data, unverified claims).
6. Explain how to properly cite and attribute evidence used in a debate case.
7. Explain the difference between statistical significance and practical/clinical significance of a finding.

D. Rebuttal & Cross-Examination Strategy (5 items)
1. Explain the purpose of a rebuttal speech in addressing the opposing team's arguments.
2. Explain effective cross-examination questioning techniques (open vs. closed questions).
3. Explain strategies for refuting an opponent's argument directly versus by comparative weighing.
4. Explain the concept of "dropping" an argument and its consequence if left unaddressed.
5. Explain strategies for maintaining composure and professionalism under cross-examination.

E. Biomedical Ethics Principles (7 items)
1. Define and apply the principle of autonomy in a patient-care or research scenario.
2. Define and apply the principle of beneficence and nonmaleficence in a healthcare ethics scenario.
3. Define and apply the principle of justice (fair distribution of benefits/burdens) in healthcare.
4. Explain the concept of informed consent as an application of autonomy.
5. Explain the role of institutional review boards (IRBs) in overseeing research ethics.
6. Explain the concept of therapeutic versus non-therapeutic research and associated ethical considerations.
7. Explain how conflicting ethical principles (e.g., autonomy vs. beneficence) create ethical dilemmas.

F. Genetic Engineering & Biotechnology Ethics (6 items)
1. Explain the ethical debate surrounding CRISPR/gene-editing technology in humans.
2. Differentiate somatic cell gene editing from germline gene editing and their differing ethical implications.
3. Explain arguments for and against genetic screening/testing (prenatal, predictive, carrier screening).
4. Explain ethical concerns related to designer babies and genetic enhancement versus therapy.
5. Explain ethical considerations in cloning and stem cell research.
6. Explain concerns about genetic privacy and discrimination (e.g., use of genetic data by insurers/employers).

G. End-of-Life Care & Resource Allocation (6 items)
1. Explain the ethical debate surrounding physician-assisted death/medical aid in dying.
2. Differentiate palliative care from hospice care and their role in end-of-life ethics debates.
3. Explain the ethical use of advance directives and living wills in end-of-life decision-making.
4. Explain triage ethics and criteria used for allocating scarce medical resources (e.g., organ transplant, crisis standards of care).
5. Explain the ethical debate over withdrawing versus withholding life-sustaining treatment.
6. Explain arguments in the debate over healthcare rationing under limited resources.

H. Healthcare Policy & Access Debate Topics (4 items)
1. Explain arguments for and against universal healthcare coverage models.
2. Explain the ethical and policy debate surrounding drug pricing and access to medications.
3. Explain arguments in the debate over vaccine mandates and public health authority versus individual rights.
4. Explain the policy debate surrounding healthcare access disparities and social determinants of health.

I. Delivery, Rhetoric & Persuasion (3 items)
1. Explain the role of ethos, pathos, and logos in persuasive debate delivery.
2. Explain the importance of clarity, pacing, and organization in oral debate delivery.
3. Explain how a team builds a cohesive narrative/theme across constructive and rebuttal speeches.
