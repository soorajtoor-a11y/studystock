HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — CLINICAL LABORATORY SCIENCE (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA Competitive Event Guidelines 2025-2026 (Round 1 written-test knowledge domain).
Format: Round 1 Written Knowledge Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (qualifies for the Round 2 skills demonstration).
Item counts per content area are proportional estimates by knowledge-area weight —
confirm against the current guideline, in parentheses.

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Knowledge of the clinical laboratory scientist/medical laboratory technician
scope of practice, including specimen collection and handling, phlebotomy,
hematology, urinalysis, clinical microbiology, immunohematology/blood banking,
and clinical chemistry, along with the safety and quality-control standards
that govern accurate laboratory testing.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Laboratory Safety & Quality Control (6 items)
1. Identify standard precautions and PPE required in a clinical laboratory (gloves, gown, face shield).
2. Describe proper handling and disposal of biohazardous and sharps waste.
3. Explain the purpose of quality control (QC) materials and control charts in verifying test accuracy.
4. Interpret a Safety Data Sheet (SDS) and chemical hazard labeling in the lab setting.
5. Describe proficiency testing and its role in laboratory accreditation (CLIA requirements).
6. Explain proper specimen labeling and chain-of-custody procedures to prevent misidentification errors.

B. Specimen Collection & Handling (7 items)
1. Identify types of specimens collected for lab testing (blood, urine, sputum, stool, swabs).
2. Describe proper patient identification procedures before specimen collection.
3. Explain requirements for specimen labeling, transport, and storage temperature.
4. Identify causes of specimen rejection (hemolysis, clotting, insufficient quantity, mislabeling).
5. Describe the order of draw for venipuncture and its purpose in preventing additive cross-contamination.
6. Explain proper centrifugation technique for separating serum/plasma from whole blood.
7. Describe factors affecting specimen integrity (time delay, temperature, light exposure).

C. Phlebotomy (6 items)
1. Identify venipuncture equipment (evacuated tubes, needles, tourniquet, butterfly needle).
2. Identify color-coded blood collection tubes and their additives (EDTA lavender, citrate blue, serum-separator gold).
3. Describe proper venipuncture site selection and technique (antecubital fossa, vein palpation).
4. Explain capillary puncture (fingerstick/heelstick) technique and appropriate use.
5. Describe complications of blood collection (hematoma, syncope, nerve injury) and how to respond.
6. Explain patient identification and consent procedures required before phlebotomy.

D. Hematology (7 items)
1. Identify components of a complete blood count (CBC): RBC, WBC, hemoglobin, hematocrit, platelet count.
2. Describe the appearance and function of the five types of white blood cells (neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils).
3. Explain the procedure and purpose of a peripheral blood smear and differential count.
4. Identify normal reference ranges and clinical significance of abnormal CBC results (anemia, leukocytosis).
5. Describe coagulation studies (PT, PTT, INR) and their clinical use in monitoring bleeding disorders.
6. Explain the purpose of erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) testing.
7. Describe proper technique for performing a manual cell count using a hemacytometer.

E. Urinalysis (6 items)
1. Describe the three components of a complete urinalysis: physical, chemical, and microscopic examination.
2. Interpret reagent (dipstick) test strip results (glucose, protein, ketones, blood, leukocyte esterase, nitrites).
3. Identify normal and abnormal physical characteristics of urine (color, clarity, specific gravity).
4. Identify microscopic urine sediment findings (RBCs, WBCs, casts, crystals, bacteria).
5. Describe proper collection methods for urine specimens (clean-catch midstream, 24-hour collection).
6. Explain the clinical significance of abnormal urinalysis findings (e.g., glucosuria, proteinuria, hematuria).

F. Clinical Microbiology & Culturing (6 items)
1. Describe aseptic technique for inoculating culture media with a patient specimen.
2. Identify common culture media types and their purposes (blood agar, MacConkey agar, chocolate agar).
3. Explain Gram stain procedure and interpret Gram-positive versus Gram-negative morphology.
4. Describe the purpose of antibiotic susceptibility (Kirby-Bauer) testing and how to interpret a zone of inhibition.
5. Identify common pathogens associated with specific infections (E. coli in UTIs, Streptococcus in strep throat).
6. Explain proper incubation conditions (temperature, atmosphere, time) for bacterial culture growth.

G. Immunohematology / Blood Banking & Typing (6 items)
1. Explain the ABO blood group system and identify antigens/antibodies for each blood type.
2. Explain Rh factor typing and its clinical significance (Rh incompatibility, Rhogam).
3. Describe the purpose and procedure of a type and crossmatch before transfusion.
4. Identify signs and symptoms of a transfusion reaction and appropriate immediate response.
5. Describe proper storage requirements and shelf life for blood products (whole blood, packed RBCs, platelets, plasma).
6. Explain the purpose of antibody screening tests in pretransfusion testing.

H. Clinical Chemistry & Instrumentation (6 items)
1. Identify common chemistry panel tests and their clinical significance (BMP, lipid panel, liver function tests).
2. Describe the principle of spectrophotometry in measuring analyte concentration.
3. Explain proper use and calibration of laboratory instruments (centrifuge, spectrophotometer, analyzer).
4. Interpret basic reference ranges for common chemistry analytes (glucose, electrolytes, creatinine).
5. Describe point-of-care testing devices and their appropriate clinical use (glucometer, blood gas analyzer).
6. Explain sources of pre-analytical, analytical, and post-analytical laboratory error.
