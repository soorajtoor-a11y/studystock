HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — EMERGENCY MEDICAL TECHNICIAN (EMT) (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA Competitive Event Guidelines 2025-2026 (Round 1 written-test knowledge domain).
Format: Round 1 Written Knowledge Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (qualifies for the team/performance round).
Item counts per content area are proportional estimates by knowledge-area weight —
confirm against the current guideline, in parentheses.
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Knowledge of prehospital emergency care at the EMT level: patient assessment,
airway/breathing/circulation management, trauma and medical emergency care,
and EMS system operations. Covers the same knowledge domain tested in the
National Registry EMT cognitive exam, scaled to a 50-item HOSA written test.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. EMS Systems & Scope of Practice (5 items)
1. Describe the roles and scope of practice of EMR, EMT, AEMT, and Paramedic levels.
2. Explain EMS system components and the chain of survival.
3. Identify legal/ethical concepts: consent (expressed, implied), refusal of care, abandonment, negligence.
4. Explain patient confidentiality requirements (HIPAA) in the prehospital setting.
5. Describe standard precautions and personal protective equipment (PPE) selection by exposure risk.

B. Patient Assessment — Primary Survey (6 items)
1. Perform a scene size-up (safety, mechanism/nature of illness, number of patients, resources).
2. Identify steps of the primary assessment (general impression, AVPU, ABCs, priority determination).
3. Differentiate a patent airway from an obstructed airway and identify immediate interventions.
4. Assess breathing adequacy (rate, depth, effort) and identify signs of inadequate ventilation.
5. Assess circulation (pulse, skin color/temperature/condition, obvious bleeding).
6. Apply the START/JumpSTART triage method in a multiple-casualty incident.

C. Patient Assessment — Secondary Survey & History (5 items)
1. Perform a focused or rapid full-body secondary assessment based on patient presentation.
2. Obtain a SAMPLE history (Signs/Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last oral intake, Events).
3. Use OPQRST to characterize a patient's chief complaint (pain/symptom).
4. Obtain and interpret baseline vital signs (pulse, respirations, blood pressure, pupils, SpO2).
5. Document findings and care in a Patient Care Report (PCR), including required legal elements.

D. Airway, Breathing & Ventilation (6 items)
1. Identify indications and techniques for manual airway maneuvers (head-tilt/chin-lift, jaw-thrust).
2. Identify indications, sizing, and insertion of oropharyngeal (OPA) and nasopharyngeal (NPA) airways.
3. Perform suctioning of the upper airway and identify suction time limits by age group.
4. Apply oxygen delivery devices appropriately (nasal cannula, non-rebreather mask, BVM) by flow rate and concentration.
5. Perform bag-valve-mask ventilation with correct rate and volume for adult, child, and infant patients.
6. Recognize signs of inadequate ventilation and hypoxia requiring immediate intervention.

E. Circulation, Bleeding & Shock (6 items)
1. Differentiate arterial, venous, and capillary bleeding by appearance.
2. Apply direct pressure, pressure dressings, and tourniquets for severe external hemorrhage control.
3. Identify signs and symptoms of compensated versus decompensated (hypovolemic) shock.
4. Explain emergency care priorities for a patient in shock, including positioning and thermal management.
5. Recognize signs of internal bleeding and appropriate transport priority.
6. Perform adult, child, and infant CPR per current guidelines, including compression rate and depth.

F. Trauma Management (7 items)
1. Apply spinal motion restriction criteria and technique based on mechanism of injury.
2. Splint suspected fractures, dislocations, and sprains using appropriate immobilization devices.
3. Manage an open chest wound (occlusive dressing) and identify signs of tension pneumothorax.
4. Manage impaled objects and evisceration injuries per standard protocol.
5. Assess and manage burns by depth, percentage (rule of nines), and critical-burn criteria.
6. Identify signs of traumatic brain injury and appropriate emergency care.
7. Explain the general approach to multi-system trauma and load-and-go versus stay-and-play decisions.

G. Medical Emergencies — Cardiac & Respiratory (5 items)
1. Recognize signs/symptoms of acute coronary syndrome (chest pain, radiation, associated symptoms).
2. Explain AED operation, indications, and safety precautions during defibrillation.
3. Differentiate asthma, COPD, and pulmonary edema presentations and their emergency care.
4. Recognize signs of a stroke and apply a stroke assessment scale (e.g., Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale).
5. Identify indications for assisting a patient with prescribed inhalers, nitroglycerin, or epinephrine auto-injectors.

H. Medical Emergencies — Neurological, Diabetic, Allergic, Toxicological (6 items)
1. Differentiate hypoglycemia from hyperglycemia by signs/symptoms and emergency care.
2. Recognize and manage generalized seizures and postictal state care.
3. Recognize signs of anaphylaxis and administer/assist with epinephrine per protocol.
4. Identify the general approach to poisoning/overdose emergencies (routes of exposure, activated charcoal, naloxone).
5. Recognize signs of altered mental status and identify common reversible causes (AEIOU-TIPS).
6. Identify signs of environmental emergencies (heat exhaustion/stroke, hypothermia, frostbite) and field treatment.

I. Special Populations (4 items)
1. Describe the stages of labor and emergency delivery procedure for an imminent birth.
2. Identify pediatric assessment differences (pediatric assessment triangle, normal vital sign ranges by age).
3. Identify geriatric assessment considerations (polypharmacy, atypical presentations, fall risk).
4. Recognize behavioral emergencies and apply verbal de-escalation and scene safety principles.
