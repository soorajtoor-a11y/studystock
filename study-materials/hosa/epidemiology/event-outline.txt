HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — EPIDEMIOLOGY (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA Competitive Event Guidelines 2025-2026 (Round 1 written-test knowledge domain).
Format: Round 1 Written Knowledge Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (qualifies for the team/performance round).
Item counts per content area are proportional estimates by knowledge-area weight —
confirm against the current guideline, in parentheses.
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Knowledge of the science of epidemiology: disease transmission and natural
history, measures of disease frequency and association, epidemiologic study
designs, outbreak investigation, surveillance systems, and levels of disease
prevention. Emphasizes quantitative reasoning as well as terminology.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Foundations & Basic Concepts (4 items)
1. Define epidemiology and distinguish descriptive from analytic epidemiology.
2. Differentiate endemic, epidemic, pandemic, outbreak, and cluster.
3. Describe the epidemiologic triangle (agent, host, environment).
4. Explain the chain of infection (reservoir, portal of exit, mode of transmission, portal of entry, susceptible host).

B. Disease Transmission & Natural History (6 items)
1. Differentiate modes of transmission: direct contact, droplet, airborne, vehicle-borne, vector-borne.
2. Explain the natural history of disease stages (exposure, latency/incubation, subclinical, clinical, resolution).
3. Differentiate incubation period from communicable period.
4. Explain herd immunity and the threshold concept for outbreak control.
5. Differentiate reservoirs of infection (human, animal/zoonotic, environmental).
6. Explain the basic reproduction number (R0) and its interpretation for transmissibility.

C. Measures of Disease Frequency (7 items)
1. Calculate and interpret incidence rate (new cases over a time period).
2. Calculate and interpret prevalence (point prevalence vs. period prevalence).
3. Differentiate incidence from prevalence and explain how disease duration affects each.
4. Calculate and interpret attack rate in an outbreak setting.
5. Calculate and interpret case fatality rate.
6. Calculate and interpret crude, specific, and adjusted (standardized) rates.
7. Interpret mortality and morbidity rate data presented in a table or graph.

D. Measures of Association & Risk (6 items)
1. Calculate and interpret relative risk (risk ratio) from a 2x2 table.
2. Calculate and interpret odds ratio from a case-control study 2x2 table.
3. Explain attributable risk and its use in public health policy decisions.
4. Interpret a confidence interval and p-value in the context of an epidemiologic finding.
5. Differentiate confounding from effect modification (interaction).
6. Identify common types of bias in epidemiologic studies (selection bias, recall bias, information bias).

E. Study Designs (7 items)
1. Differentiate cohort studies (prospective/retrospective) from case-control studies.
2. Differentiate cross-sectional studies from longitudinal studies.
3. Explain the structure and purpose of a randomized controlled trial (RCT).
4. Identify strengths and limitations of ecological (population-level) studies.
5. Explain the purpose and structure of a systematic review/meta-analysis.
6. Identify which study design is best suited to a rare disease versus a rare exposure.
7. Differentiate experimental from observational study designs.

F. Outbreak Investigation Steps (7 items)
1. Sequence the standard steps of an outbreak investigation (confirm diagnosis, establish case definition, etc.).
2. Construct and interpret a case definition (person, place, time criteria).
3. Interpret an epidemic curve (epi curve) to identify a point-source versus propagated outbreak.
4. Explain descriptive epidemiology's "person, place, time" framework as applied to outbreak data.
5. Explain the purpose of generating and testing hypotheses about exposure source during an investigation.
6. Explain when and why control measures are implemented during an active investigation.
7. Explain the role of communicating findings to stakeholders and the public after an investigation.

G. Surveillance Systems (5 items)
1. Differentiate active from passive disease surveillance.
2. Identify the purpose of nationally notifiable disease reporting.
3. Describe the role of the CDC and state/local health departments in disease surveillance.
4. Explain sentinel surveillance and syndromic surveillance systems.
5. Explain the purpose of vital statistics registries (births, deaths, causes of death) in surveillance.

H. Screening & Diagnostic Testing (4 items)
1. Calculate and interpret sensitivity and specificity of a screening test.
2. Calculate and interpret positive predictive value and negative predictive value.
3. Explain how disease prevalence affects predictive value of a test.
4. Explain criteria for an effective screening program (e.g., Wilson-Jungner criteria).

I. Prevention Levels & Public Health Application (4 items)
1. Differentiate primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention with concrete examples of each.
2. Explain the role of vaccination programs in primary prevention and disease eradication.
3. Explain how epidemiologic data inform public health policy decisions.
4. Describe historical epidemiologic milestones (e.g., John Snow and the Broad Street pump).
