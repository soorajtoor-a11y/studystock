HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — FORENSIC SCIENCE (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA Competitive Event Guidelines 2025-2026 (Round 1 written-test knowledge domain).
Format: Round 1 Written Knowledge Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (qualifies for the team/quiz-bowl round).
Item counts per content area are proportional estimates by knowledge-area weight —
confirm against the current guideline, in parentheses.
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Knowledge of the scientific principles and procedures used to collect,
preserve, and analyze physical evidence in the investigation of a crime.
Covers crime scene processing and chain of custody, pattern and trace
evidence (fingerprints, DNA, ballistics, toxicology), forensic pathology,
forensic anthropology and odontology, laboratory analysis techniques, and
the legal/courtroom use of forensic evidence.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Crime Scene Processing & Chain of Custody (8 items)
1. Sequence the steps of processing a crime scene (secure, document,
   collect, preserve, transport evidence).
2. Explain the purpose of establishing and maintaining chain of custody for
   physical evidence.
3. Identify methods of crime scene documentation (photography, sketching,
   note-taking, videography).
4. Differentiate primary from secondary crime scenes.
5. Identify proper packaging and labeling procedures for different evidence
   types (paper bags for biological evidence, airtight containers for
   accelerants).
6. Explain the concept of cross-contamination and how investigators prevent
   it (gloves, evidence markers, single-use tools).
7. Identify Locard's exchange principle and its role in trace evidence
   collection.
8. Explain the legal requirement of a search warrant for processing certain
   crime scenes.

B. Fingerprint Analysis (6 items)
1. Identify the three basic fingerprint patterns (loop, whorl, arch).
2. Differentiate patent, latent, and plastic fingerprints by how they are
   formed and visualized.
3. Identify common latent print development techniques (dusting with
   powder, ninhydrin, cyanoacrylate/superglue fuming).
4. Explain the concept of minutiae points used to match fingerprints.
5. Explain how AFIS (Automated Fingerprint Identification System) is used
   to search for matches.
6. Explain why no two individuals (including identical twins) share
   identical fingerprints.

C. DNA Evidence & Serology (7 items)
1. Identify sources of biological evidence that yield DNA (blood, saliva,
   hair follicles, skin cells, semen).
2. Explain the process of PCR (polymerase chain reaction) in amplifying
   DNA samples.
3. Explain how STR (short tandem repeat) analysis is used to generate a DNA
   profile.
4. Differentiate nuclear DNA from mitochondrial DNA and when each is used.
5. Explain the purpose of CODIS (Combined DNA Index System) in criminal
   investigations.
6. Identify presumptive tests used in serology (Kastle-Meyer test for
   blood).
7. Explain the concept of touch DNA and its evidentiary limitations.

D. Ballistics & Firearms Evidence (5 items)
1. Differentiate class characteristics from individual characteristics of a
   firearm (rifling/lands and grooves versus unique tool marks).
2. Explain how a bullet's rifling marks are matched to a specific firearm.
3. Identify the purpose of gunshot residue (GSR) testing.
4. Explain how trajectory analysis is used to reconstruct a shooting scene.
5. Identify the difference between entrance and exit wound characteristics
   relevant to firearms evidence.

E. Toxicology (5 items)
1. Define forensic toxicology and its role in determining cause of death or
   impairment.
2. Identify common biological specimens used in toxicology testing (blood,
   urine, vitreous humor, hair).
3. Differentiate a screening test from a confirmatory test (immunoassay
   versus gas chromatography-mass spectrometry).
4. Explain the concept of postmortem redistribution affecting drug
   concentration readings.
5. Identify common classes of substances tested for in a toxicology panel
   (alcohol, opioids, stimulants).

F. Forensic Pathology (Cause & Manner of Death) (6 items)
1. Differentiate cause of death from manner of death (natural, accident,
   suicide, homicide, undetermined).
2. Identify the purpose and general procedure of an autopsy.
3. Explain how postmortem interval (time since death) is estimated (livor
   mortis, rigor mortis, algor mortis, decomposition stage).
4. Differentiate livor mortis, rigor mortis, and algor mortis.
5. Identify the role of a medical examiner versus a coroner.
6. Explain how wound pattern analysis helps determine the mechanism of
   death.

G. Forensic Anthropology & Odontology (5 items)
1. Explain how forensic anthropologists estimate age, sex, ancestry, and
   stature from skeletal remains.
2. Identify skeletal features used to distinguish male from female
   remains (pelvis shape, skull features).
3. Explain how bite-mark analysis is used in forensic odontology.
4. Explain how dental records are used to identify unknown remains.
5. Identify limitations and controversies of bite-mark comparison as
   forensic evidence.

H. Trace Evidence & Lab Techniques (4 items)
1. Identify common types of trace evidence (hair, fibers, glass, soil,
   paint chips).
2. Explain how microscopy is used to compare hair and fiber evidence.
3. Explain the purpose of chromatography in separating and identifying
   chemical substances.
4. Explain how spectrometry (e.g., mass spectrometry) identifies unknown
   substances found at a scene.

I. Legal/Courtroom Aspects of Forensic Evidence (4 items)
1. Explain the role of an expert witness in presenting forensic evidence in
   court.
2. Identify the standard used to determine admissibility of scientific
   evidence (Daubert or Frye standard).
3. Explain why a broken chain of custody can result in evidence being ruled
   inadmissible.
4. Explain the difference between circumstantial and direct evidence in a
   criminal case.
