HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — MATH FOR HEALTH PROFESSIONALS (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA / NGL Academic Testing Center event; content follows a standard
health-sciences mathematics curriculum (clinical calculation focus).
Format: Objective Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (+ tiebreaker).
Item counts per topic area are proportional estimates — confirm against the
current event guideline.
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Mathematical concepts and computational skills required in health-care
settings, from basic arithmetic and measurement systems through dosage
calculation, IV flow rates, and interpretation of clinical data.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Basic Arithmetic & Number Systems (4 items)
1. Perform operations with whole numbers, integers, and signed numbers.
2. Round numbers and estimate to check reasonableness of a calculated answer.
3. Apply order of operations to multi-step numeric expressions.
4. Convert between standard and scientific notation.

B. Fractions, Decimals & Percentages (5 items)
1. Add, subtract, multiply, and divide fractions and mixed numbers.
2. Convert among fractions, decimals, and percentages.
3. Calculate percentage strength of a solution and percentage change.
4. Solve problems involving discounts, markups, and error rate percentages.
5. Round decimals appropriately for clinical measurements (e.g., to the tenth).

C. Ratio and Proportion (5 items)
1. Set up and solve a ratio-proportion (means-extremes) for an unknown quantity.
2. Express drug concentrations as ratios (e.g., 1:1000 epinephrine).
3. Solve proportion problems involving scale and dilution.
4. Apply cross-multiplication to verify equivalent ratios.
5. Convert ratio strength to percentage strength and vice versa.

D. Systems of Measurement (Metric, Household, Apothecary) (5 items)
1. Identify base units of the metric system (gram, liter, meter) and common prefixes (kilo-, milli-, micro-).
2. Convert within the metric system using unit prefixes.
3. Convert between metric and household measures (mL to teaspoons/tablespoons, kg to lb).
4. Identify apothecary and household units still referenced in older orders (grain, dram, ounce).
5. Convert Fahrenheit and Celsius temperature scales.

E. Dimensional Analysis / Unit Conversion (6 items)
1. Set up a dimensional analysis (factor-label) equation with conversion factors.
2. Cancel units correctly across multiple conversion factors in a single equation.
3. Convert compound units (e.g., mg/kg/day to mg/dose).
4. Solve multi-step conversion problems combining weight, volume, and time units.
5. Identify and correct unit-cancellation errors in a worked dimensional analysis problem.
6. Apply dimensional analysis to convert between household and metric volume units.

F. Dosage Calculations — Oral & Parenteral (7 items)
1. Apply the desired-over-have (D/H x Q) formula to calculate oral tablet or liquid dosage.
2. Calculate the number of tablets or capsules needed from a prescribed dose.
3. Calculate injectable dosage from a stock (available) concentration.
4. Convert a prescribed dose in mg/kg to a total dose based on patient weight.
5. Calculate dosage when the order and the label are in different units (e.g., grains vs. mg).
6. Determine safe dosage range and compare a prescribed dose against it.
7. Calculate reconstitution volume and resulting concentration for a powdered drug.

G. IV Flow Rates & Drip Calculations (6 items)
1. Calculate IV flow rate in mL/hr from a total volume and infusion time.
2. Calculate drops per minute (gtt/min) using the drop factor of the administration set.
3. Convert between mL/hr and gtt/min using the tubing's drop factor.
4. Calculate infusion time remaining given a flow rate and volume remaining.
5. Calculate a medication drip rate in mcg/kg/min for a titrated IV medication.
6. Recalculate an IV flow rate after a change in ordered volume or time.

H. Pediatric Dosage Calculations (4 items)
1. Calculate a pediatric dose using body weight (mg/kg).
2. Calculate a pediatric dose using body surface area (BSA) with the appropriate formula.
3. Apply Clark's rule or a similar weight-based pediatric dosage rule.
4. Determine whether a calculated pediatric dose falls within the recommended safe range.

I. Basic Algebra Applied to Healthcare (4 items)
1. Solve a linear equation for an unknown clinical variable.
2. Calculate Body Mass Index (BMI) using the standard formula and interpret the result.
3. Solve formula-based problems involving rate, distance/volume, and time.
4. Apply direct and inverse variation to healthcare quantities (e.g., concentration and volume).

J. Statistics & Data Interpretation in Healthcare (4 items)
1. Calculate mean, median, mode, and range from a set of clinical data.
2. Interpret data presented in tables, bar graphs, and line graphs of vital signs or lab values.
3. Calculate a simple rate or ratio from population/clinical data (e.g., infection rate per 1,000).
4. Interpret basic probability in the context of screening test results.
