HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — MEDICAL READING (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA Competitive Event Guidelines 2025-2026 (official test plan).
Format: Objective Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (10 questions
per reference book) (+ tiebreakers).
Item counts per content area (from the official test-plan percentages) shown in
(parentheses).
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Reading comprehension applied to health and medical passages: identifying
main idea and author's purpose, drawing inferences, interpreting vocabulary
in context, following multi-step written instructions (e.g., medication
package inserts, patient discharge instructions), reading charts and tables
embedded in a health passage, and distinguishing fact from opinion in health
reporting and journalism.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Main Idea, Theme & Author's Purpose (8 items)
1. Identify the central/main idea of a health-related passage or paragraph.
2. Distinguish a passage's main idea from a supporting detail.
3. Identify the author's primary purpose (to inform, persuade, entertain, instruct).
4. Identify the intended audience of a health passage (patient, clinician, general public).
5. Summarize a multi-paragraph passage in one sentence without adding outside information.
6. Identify the best title for a given passage based on its content.
7. Recognize a shift in topic or focus between paragraphs.
8. Distinguish the author's tone (e.g., clinical/objective, urgent, reassuring, critical).

B. Inference & Drawing Conclusions (8 items)
1. Draw a logical conclusion from stated facts in a passage without restating the text.
2. Identify an implied cause-and-effect relationship not explicitly stated.
3. Predict a likely outcome based on details given in a passage.
4. Identify an unstated assumption the author is making.
5. Determine what a character or patient in a narrative passage is likely feeling or thinking based on context clues.
6. Identify information that would strengthen or weaken a claim made in the passage.
7. Distinguish a valid inference from an unsupported overgeneralization.
8. Identify the most reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous statement given surrounding context.

C. Vocabulary in Context (8 items)
1. Determine the meaning of an unfamiliar medical or general term from surrounding context clues.
2. Identify the meaning of a multiple-meaning word as used in a specific sentence (e.g., "chart," "discharge," "positive").
3. Identify a word or phrase's connotation (positive, negative, neutral) as used in context.
4. Select the best synonym for a word as it is used in a given sentence.
5. Identify how a transitional word or phrase (however, therefore, in contrast, consequently) signals the relationship between ideas.
6. Interpret figurative language (metaphor, simile, idiom) used in a health-related narrative.
7. Identify the meaning of an acronym or abbreviation defined earlier in a passage (e.g., BP, IV, PRN, NPO).
8. Determine the most precise word choice an author could substitute without changing meaning.

D. Following Multi-Step Written Instructions (8 items)
1. Sequence the correct order of steps given in a set of patient-care or medication instructions.
2. Identify a required precondition stated before an instructed action (e.g., "take with food," "shake well before use").
3. Interpret dosage and frequency language on a medication label (e.g., "every 4-6 hours as needed, not to exceed 4 doses in 24 hours").
4. Identify a contraindication or warning stated in a drug package insert.
5. Identify the correct action when two instructions appear to conflict, based on stated priority language.
6. Interpret conditional instructions ("if symptoms persist beyond 3 days, contact your physician").
7. Identify what information a patient must report to a provider based on listed discharge instructions.
8. Distinguish a mandatory instruction from an optional recommendation based on wording (must/shall vs. may/should).

E. Interpreting Charts, Tables & Graphs in Health Passages (8 items)
1. Locate a specific data point within a table or chart embedded in a passage.
2. Compare two or more values across rows or columns of a data table.
3. Identify a trend shown in a line graph or bar chart accompanying a health passage.
4. Calculate a simple difference, sum, or percentage change from data presented in a table.
5. Match a statement in the passage text to the supporting data shown in an accompanying figure.
6. Identify which axis, label, or unit a value in a chart refers to.
7. Determine whether a claim made in the passage is supported or contradicted by the data shown.
8. Identify the correct row/category when a table uses abbreviated or coded labels.

F. Fact vs. Opinion in Health Reporting (5 items)
1. Distinguish a verifiable factual statement from an opinion or value judgment in a health news passage.
2. Identify a statement that reflects bias rather than objective reporting.
3. Identify loaded or emotionally charged language used to influence the reader.
4. Distinguish a researcher's finding (data-based) from a journalist's interpretation of that finding.
5. Identify a statement that requires a citation or source to be considered a verified fact.

G. Text Structure & Organizational Patterns (5 items)
1. Identify a passage's organizational pattern (chronological, cause-effect, compare-contrast, problem-solution).
2. Identify the topic sentence of a given paragraph.
3. Identify how a concluding paragraph relates to the passage's introduction.
4. Recognize when an author is comparing/contrasting two conditions, treatments, or time periods.
5. Identify supporting evidence the author uses to back a claim (statistic, anecdote, expert quote, example).
