HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — NUTRITION (ACADEMIC) (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA / NGL Academic Testing Center event; content follows a standard
introductory college nutrition science curriculum.
Format: Objective Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (+ tiebreaker).
Item counts per topic area are proportional estimates — confirm against the
current event guideline.
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Biochemical and physiological basis of human nutrition, covering the
macronutrients and micronutrients, energy metabolism, digestion and
absorption, nutritional assessment, needs across the lifespan, and the
relationship between diet and chronic disease.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Foundations of Nutrition Science (3 items)
1. Define the six classes of nutrients and distinguish essential from nonessential nutrients.
2. Describe the Dietary Reference Intakes (EAR, RDA, AI, UL) and their uses.
3. Interpret the Dietary Guidelines for Americans and MyPlate recommendations.

B. Carbohydrates (4 items)
1. Classify carbohydrates by structure (monosaccharides, disaccharides, polysaccharides).
2. Differentiate simple and complex carbohydrates and describe dietary sources.
3. Explain the glycemic index/glycemic load concept and its physiological relevance.
4. Describe the role of dietary fiber in digestion and disease prevention.

C. Lipids (4 items)
1. Classify lipids by structure (triglycerides, phospholipids, sterols).
2. Differentiate saturated, unsaturated, and trans fatty acids and their health effects.
3. Describe the role of lipoproteins (LDL, HDL, VLDL) in lipid transport.
4. Identify essential fatty acids and their dietary sources and functions.

D. Proteins (4 items)
1. Describe amino acid structure and classify essential versus nonessential amino acids.
2. Explain protein quality concepts (complete vs. incomplete protein, complementary proteins).
3. Describe the roles of protein in the body (structural, enzymatic, transport, immune).
4. Explain nitrogen balance and its clinical significance.

E. Energy Metabolism & Energy Balance (5 items)
1. Calculate energy content of a food from its macronutrient composition (4-4-9 kcal rule).
2. Describe components of total energy expenditure (BMR, thermic effect of food, physical activity).
3. Explain the biochemical pathways of glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and beta-oxidation at an overview level.
4. Differentiate anabolism and catabolism.
5. Explain the physiological basis of energy imbalance (weight gain versus weight loss).

F. Vitamins (5 items)
1. Differentiate fat-soluble and water-soluble vitamins by absorption and storage.
2. Identify major functions and deficiency diseases of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).
3. Identify major functions and deficiency diseases of water-soluble vitamins (B-complex, C).
4. Describe the role of vitamin D in calcium homeostasis and bone health.
5. Identify populations at risk for specific vitamin deficiencies.

G. Minerals & Water Balance (5 items)
1. Differentiate major minerals and trace minerals and identify their key functions.
2. Describe the role of calcium and phosphorus in bone health.
3. Describe the role of iron in oxygen transport and identify causes of iron-deficiency anemia.
4. Explain sodium and potassium's roles in fluid balance and blood pressure regulation.
5. Describe mechanisms of fluid balance and consequences of dehydration and overhydration.

H. Digestion & Absorption Physiology (5 items)
1. Describe the digestive processes occurring in the mouth, stomach, and small intestine.
2. Identify the roles of major digestive enzymes and secretions (amylase, pepsin, lipase, bile).
3. Describe the structure of the small intestine that maximizes nutrient absorption (villi, microvilli).
4. Explain absorption pathways for carbohydrates, fats, and proteins.
5. Describe the role of the large intestine in water absorption and fiber fermentation.

I. Nutritional Assessment Methods (4 items)
1. Describe the ABCD approach to nutritional assessment (Anthropometric, Biochemical, Clinical, Dietary).
2. Calculate and interpret Body Mass Index (BMI) and its limitations.
3. Describe common dietary assessment tools (24-hour recall, food frequency questionnaire, food diary).
4. Interpret biochemical markers used to assess nutritional status (serum albumin, hemoglobin, ferritin).

J. Nutrition Across the Lifespan (5 items)
1. Describe nutritional needs and concerns during pregnancy and lactation.
2. Describe nutritional needs of infants and rationale for breastfeeding recommendations.
3. Describe nutritional considerations for childhood and adolescent growth.
4. Describe nutritional changes and concerns in older adulthood.
5. Explain the role of nutrition in athletic performance and hydration needs.

K. Diet-Disease Relationships & Public Health Nutrition (6 items)
1. Explain the relationship between diet and cardiovascular disease risk factors.
2. Explain the role of diet in the development and management of type 2 diabetes.
3. Describe dietary approaches to weight management and obesity prevention.
4. Identify common food allergies and intolerances and their physiological mechanisms.
5. Describe the concept of food security and its public health implications.
6. Explain principles of food safety relevant to nutrition (foodborne illness prevention).
