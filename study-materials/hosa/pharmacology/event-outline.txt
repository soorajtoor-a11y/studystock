HOSA STUDY OUTLINE — PHARMACOLOGY (2025–2026)
Source: HOSA Competitive Event Guidelines 2025-2026 (official test plan).
Format: Objective Test — 50 multiple-choice questions, 60 minutes (+ tiebreakers).
Item counts per content area (from the official test-plan percentages) shown in
(parentheses).
============================================================

EVENT DESCRIPTION
Knowledge of pharmacology: drug classifications and mechanisms of action,
pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics, routes of administration and dosage
forms, dosage calculations, common medications by body system, adverse
effects and interactions, controlled substance scheduling, and safe
medication administration.

KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES

A. Pharmacokinetics — Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion (5 items)
1. Define and sequence the four phases of pharmacokinetics (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion).
2. Explain factors affecting drug absorption (route, first-pass metabolism, food interactions).
3. Explain the concept of drug half-life and its effect on dosing frequency.
4. Explain the role of the liver (cytochrome P450 enzymes) in drug metabolism.
5. Explain the role of the kidneys in drug excretion and how renal impairment affects dosing.

B. Pharmacodynamics & Mechanisms of Action (4 items)
1. Differentiate agonist from antagonist drug action at a receptor.
2. Explain the concept of therapeutic index and narrow therapeutic range drugs (e.g., warfarin, digoxin, lithium).
3. Explain the difference between drug potency and drug efficacy.
4. Explain the concept of a dose-response relationship and ceiling effect.

C. Routes of Administration & Dosage Forms (4 items)
1. Differentiate enteral routes (oral, sublingual, buccal, rectal) by relative onset of action.
2. Differentiate parenteral routes (intravenous, intramuscular, subcutaneous, intradermal) by onset and typical use.
3. Identify appropriate use of topical/transdermal, inhalation, and ophthalmic/otic routes.
4. Differentiate dosage forms (tablet, capsule, suspension, extended-release, enteric-coated) and their administration considerations.

D. Dosage Calculations & Drug Measurement (4 items)
1. Calculate a drug dose using the desired-over-have (D/H x Q) method.
2. Calculate an IV flow rate given volume, time, and drop factor.
3. Convert between common units of measurement (mg to mcg, mL to L, grains to mg).
4. Calculate a pediatric dose using body weight (mg/kg) given a prescribed dosing range.

E. Antibiotics & Anti-Infectives (4 items)
1. Identify the mechanism of action of a specified antibiotic class (e.g., penicillins/cell wall synthesis, macrolides/protein synthesis).
2. Identify indications and common adverse effects of a specified antibiotic class (e.g., fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines, aminoglycosides).
3. Explain the concept of antibiotic resistance and appropriate antibiotic stewardship.
4. Identify the mechanism/use of a specified antiviral, antifungal, or antiparasitic agent.

F. Cardiovascular & Renal Drugs (5 items)
1. Identify the mechanism of action and use of a specified antihypertensive class (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers).
2. Identify the mechanism of action and nursing considerations of a specified anticoagulant/antiplatelet (warfarin, heparin, aspirin, clopidogrel).
3. Identify the mechanism of action and use of a specified diuretic class (loop, thiazide, potassium-sparing).
4. Identify the mechanism of action and adverse effects of cardiac glycosides (digoxin) and antiarrhythmics.
5. Identify the mechanism of action and use of statins and other lipid-lowering agents.

G. Analgesics, Anesthetics & CNS Drugs (5 items)
1. Differentiate opioid from nonopioid (NSAID, acetaminophen) analgesics by mechanism and risk profile.
2. Identify signs of opioid overdose and the mechanism of action of naloxone as a reversal agent.
3. Differentiate local, regional, and general anesthesia by mechanism and use.
4. Identify the mechanism/use of a specified CNS drug class (anticonvulsants, benzodiazepines, antidepressants, antipsychotics).
5. Identify common adverse effects/black box warnings of a specified CNS medication class.

H. Endocrine & Reproductive Drugs (4 items)
1. Identify the mechanism of action and administration considerations of insulin and oral antidiabetic agents (e.g., metformin, sulfonylureas).
2. Identify the mechanism of action and use of corticosteroids, including adverse effects of long-term use.
3. Identify the mechanism of action and use of thyroid replacement and antithyroid medications.
4. Identify the mechanism of action and use of hormonal contraceptives and reproductive hormone therapies.

I. Respiratory & GI Drugs (4 items)
1. Identify the mechanism of action and use of bronchodilators (beta-2 agonists, anticholinergics) and inhaled corticosteroids.
2. Identify the mechanism of action and use of antihistamines and decongestants.
3. Identify the mechanism of action and use of proton pump inhibitors, H2 blockers, and antacids.
4. Identify the mechanism of action and use of antiemetics and laxatives/antidiarrheals.

J. Psychotropic Drugs & Controlled Substance Schedules (4 items)
1. Differentiate DEA controlled substance schedules I-V by abuse potential and accepted medical use, with an example drug in each.
2. Identify legal/regulatory requirements for prescribing and dispensing scheduled medications.
3. Identify signs of physical dependence vs. tolerance vs. addiction.
4. Identify appropriate safeguards for storage and administration of controlled substances in a clinical setting.

K. Adverse Effects, Interactions & Contraindications (4 items)
1. Differentiate a side effect from an adverse drug reaction and from an allergic reaction.
2. Identify a drug-drug interaction and its mechanism (e.g., additive, synergistic, antagonistic).
3. Identify a drug-food interaction (e.g., warfarin and vitamin K, MAOIs and tyramine, grapefruit juice and statins).
4. Identify a contraindication for a specified drug given a patient condition (e.g., beta-blockers in asthma, NSAIDs in renal impairment).

L. Drug Regulation, Safety & Administration Rights (3 items)
1. Identify the role of the FDA in drug approval and the phases of clinical drug trials.
2. Apply the "rights" of medication administration (right patient, drug, dose, route, time, documentation).
3. Identify correct procedure for reporting and documenting a medication error.
