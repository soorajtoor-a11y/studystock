FBLA STUDY CONTENT — INTRODUCTION TO PARLIAMENTARY PROCEDURE (2025–2026)
LONG-FORM EDITION
Based on Robert's Rules of Order Newly Revised (RONR, the event's
official handbook). Test = 100 questions: ~90 parliamentary procedure
+ ~10 FBLA bylaws. Parli pro questions are drawn from the National
Association of Parliamentarians (NAP) official test bank, so this
document is built to NAP-exam depth.

CONSTRUCTED KNOWLEDGE AREAS AND OBJECTIVES
(FBLA publishes no objective list for this event; this path is
modeled on the official format, built from RONR and NAP exam scope.)

A. Principles and Foundations of Parliamentary Law
1. State the fundamental principles of parliamentary law.
2. Define parliamentary authority, bylaws, standing rules, special rules of order.
3. Distinguish a session from a meeting; define quorum and its consequences.
B. Classification and Precedence of Motions
1. Classify any motion: main, subsidiary, privileged, incidental, bring-back.
2. Apply the 13-motion order of precedence to determine if a motion is in order.
C. Rules for Each Motion
1. For any motion, state: second required? debatable? amendable? vote required? can it be reconsidered?
2. Identify the purpose and correct use of each motion.
D. Processing Business
1. List the six steps in handling a main motion.
2. Apply the rules of debate and recognition.
3. Apply amendment rules, including degrees and forms of amendment.
E. Voting
1. Differentiate voting methods and when each is used.
2. Compute majority and two-thirds outcomes; handle ties correctly.
F. Meetings, Officers, and Records
1. Recite the standard order of business.
2. State the duties of president, vice president, secretary, treasurer, parliamentarian.
3. Identify what belongs (and doesn't belong) in minutes.
G. Committees, Nominations, and Elections
1. Distinguish standing vs. special committees and committee procedure.
2. Apply nomination methods and election rules.
H. FBLA Bylaws and Organization
1. State FBLA's divisions, regions, officers, and key governance rules.

PART 1 — FOUNDATIONS

1.1 Fundamental principles of parliamentary law
- One question at a time; one speaker at a time.
- The majority decides, but only after the minority has been heard.
- Every member has equal rights, privileges, and obligations.
- The rights of the assembly, the minority, individual members, and
  absentees must all be protected.
- Justice, courtesy, and impartiality for all; partiality to none.
- Silence gives consent — members who abstain agree to abide by the
  result of the vote.
- Purpose of parliamentary procedure: to transact business
  efficiently while protecting the rights of every member.

1.2 Governing documents (from highest authority down)
1. Corporate charter (if incorporated)
2. Bylaws — the fundamental rules of the organization. CANNOT be
   suspended. Amended only with previous notice + 2/3 vote (typical).
3. Special rules of order — modify the parliamentary authority;
   adopted by 2/3 with notice; suspendable by 2/3.
4. Rules of order / parliamentary authority (RONR for FBLA) —
   applies wherever bylaws are silent.
5. Standing rules — administrative details (e.g., meeting time);
   adopted by majority; suspended by majority for a session.
Custom falls to the ground when in conflict with written rules.

1.3 Sessions, meetings, quorum
- MEETING: a single official gathering with no break longer than a
  recess. SESSION: one or more connected meetings devoted to one
  order of business (a convention is one session).
- QUORUM: the minimum number of members who must be present to
  transact business legally. Default: a majority of the membership,
  unless the bylaws set another number.
- Without a quorum, the only actions in order are: fix the time to
  which to adjourn, adjourn, recess, and measures to obtain a quorum.
  Any other business transacted is NULL AND VOID.
- The chair should declare the presence of a quorum before business;
  any member may raise a point of order about quorum.

PART 2 — THE MOTIONS SYSTEM

2.1 The five classes
- MAIN MOTION: brings business before the assembly. Only one main
  motion may be pending at a time; lowest rank. Two types: original
  main motions (new substantive business) and incidental main
  motions (procedural business, e.g., ratify, adopt a recommendation).
- SUBSIDIARY MOTIONS: help dispose of the main motion (amend it,
  delay it, kill it, send it to committee, control its debate).
- PRIVILEGED MOTIONS: urgent matters unrelated to the pending
  question (adjourning, recessing, comfort of the assembly).
- INCIDENTAL MOTIONS: procedural questions arising out of pending
  business; decided immediately; no rank among themselves.
- MOTIONS THAT BRING A QUESTION AGAIN BEFORE THE ASSEMBLY:
  take from the table, reconsider, rescind/amend something
  previously adopted, discharge a committee.

2.2 Order of precedence (memorize cold: #1 highest to #13 lowest)
 1. Fix the time to which to adjourn      (privileged)
 2. Adjourn                                (privileged)
 3. Recess                                 (privileged)
 4. Raise a question of privilege         (privileged)
 5. Call for the orders of the day        (privileged)
 6. Lay on the table                       (subsidiary)
 7. Previous question                      (subsidiary)
 8. Limit or extend limits of debate      (subsidiary)
 9. Postpone to a certain time            (subsidiary)
 10. Commit or refer                       (subsidiary)
 11. Amend                                 (subsidiary)
 12. Postpone indefinitely                 (subsidiary)
 13. Main motion
RULES: When a motion is pending, any motion HIGHER on the list is in
order; any motion LOWER is out of order. Motions are voted on in
REVERSE order of their making (highest precedence first).

2.3 The complete motions chart
(S=Second req'd, D=Debatable, A=Amendable, V=Vote, R=Reconsiderable)

Adjourn                       S:Y  D:N  A:N  V:Majority   R:N
Recess                        S:Y  D:N  A:Y  V:Majority   R:N
Raise question of privilege   S:N  D:N  A:N  V:Chair rules R:N

SUBSIDIARY

Limit/extend debate           S:Y  D:N  A:Y  V:2/3        R:Y

  (*debatable only when the motion to be amended is debatable)

  TIE SUSTAINS THE CHAIR  (*undebatable if it concerns decorum,

Requests (parliamentary inquiry, request for information, withdraw
  a motion, read papers): decided by chair or majority.
  - Withdraw: before the chair states the motion, the maker may
    withdraw freely; after, requires consent of the assembly.

  (in order until the end of the NEXT regular session)
Reconsider                    S:Y  D:*  A:N  V:Majority
  (*debatable when the motion to be reconsidered is debatable)
  - May be moved ONLY by a member who voted on the PREVAILING side.
  - May be made only on the same day or the next day of the session.
  - Suspends action on the decision until resolved.
Rescind / Amend something

  V: 2/3 without notice, OR majority WITH previous notice, OR
  majority of the ENTIRE membership.
  - Cannot rescind action already carried out that can't be undone.
Discharge a committee         S:Y  D:Y  A:Y  V: same as rescind

2.4 Purposes — what each motion is FOR
- Postpone indefinitely: KILLS the main motion for the session
  without a direct vote on it (also lets opponents test strength).
- Amend: modify wording. Forms: (1) insert/add, (2) strike out,
  (3) strike out and insert / substitute.
- Commit/refer: send to a committee for study or redrafting.
- Postpone to a certain time: delay to a definite time (becomes a
  general or special order).
- Limit/extend debate: change the 10-minute/2-speech defaults.
- Previous question: immediately close debate and vote. "Calling
  the question" from your seat has NO effect — it must be moved,
  seconded, and adopted by 2/3.
- Lay on the table: set business aside TEMPORARILY for something
  urgent. Misusing it to kill a motion is improper — use postpone
  indefinitely instead.
- Question of privilege: comfort/safety/rights issues (too noisy,
  can't hear, confidential matter -> enter executive session).
- Orders of the day: demand return to the scheduled agenda.
- Suspend the rules: do something the rules of order prevent
  (never bylaws, never protections of absentees/minority).
- Division of the assembly: verify an inconclusive voice vote by
  rising vote — any single member may demand it.
- Division of the question: split a motion with independent parts
  into separate questions.

2.5 Amendment rules in depth
- Amendments must be GERMANE (relevant) to the motion.
- First-degree amendment: amends the main motion.
  Second-degree amendment: amends the first-degree amendment.
  THIRD-degree amendments are NOT allowed.
- Vote order: second-degree first, then first-degree, then the main
  motion as amended.
- An amendment may be hostile (against the spirit) yet still
  germane — that is allowed.
- Adopting an amendment does NOT adopt the main motion; the main
  motion (as amended) must still be voted on.
- "Friendly amendment": after the chair states the motion, it still
  requires normal amendment processing or unanimous consent — the
  maker alone cannot accept it.

PART 3 — RUNNING BUSINESS

3.1 Six steps of a main motion
1. Member rises and addresses the chair ("Madam/Mr. President").
2. Chair recognizes the member (member "has the floor").
3. Member states the motion: "I move that ..."
   (NEVER "I make a motion that" / "I so move" is improper form.)
4. Another member seconds ("Second!") — shows at least two members
   want it considered. Committee-reported motions need NO second.
   A motion that gets no second simply does not come before the
   assembly (it is not "dead on the floor" — it just isn't taken up).
5. Chair STATES the question ("It is moved and seconded that ...").
   Only now does the motion belong to the assembly.
6. Debate (if debatable) -> chair PUTS the question (takes the
   vote) -> chair ANNOUNCES the result and what happens next
   ("The ayes have it, the motion is adopted, and ...").

3.2 Debate rules (RONR defaults)
- Only the member recognized by the chair may speak.
- Limit: TWO speeches per member, per motion, per day; TEN minutes
  each. (Changing this requires limit/extend debate, 2/3.)
- The maker of the motion has the right to speak FIRST in debate
  and may not speak AGAINST their own motion (may vote against it).
- No member speaks a second time while another who hasn't spoken
  seeks the floor.
- Debate must be germane; address the chair, not other members;
  no attacking motives; refer to members impersonally ("the
  previous speaker") — no names in debate.
- The presiding officer must be IMPARTIAL: does not enter debate
  while presiding (must relinquish the chair to debate, and does
  not return until the question is disposed of).

3.3 Voting
- Methods: voice (viva voce) — the normal method for majority
  votes; rising — required to verify on demand and for 2/3 votes;
  show of hands (small groups); ballot (secrecy — required when
  bylaws say so, e.g., elections); roll call (records each member's
  vote — used in representative bodies); unanimous/general consent
  ("If there is no objection ..." — a single objection forces a
  regular vote); absentee/mail/proxy only if bylaws authorize.
- MAJORITY VOTE = more than half of the votes CAST by members
  entitled to vote (blanks/abstentions are ignored).
  Example: 19 votes cast -> majority is 10.
- TWO-THIRDS VOTE = at least 2/3 of votes cast.
  Example: 60 votes cast -> need 40.
- A TIE DEFEATS a motion (majority not achieved). EXCEPTION: on an
  appeal, a tie SUSTAINS the chair's decision.
- The chair, if a member, votes when the vote is by ballot, or
  whenever their vote would CHANGE the result (to make or break a
  tie) — never twice.
- An abstention is not a vote; members are not compelled to vote.
- No member may vote on a question of direct personal or pecuniary
  interest not shared by other members.

3.4 Standard order of business
1. Call to order ("The meeting will come to order.")
2. Opening ceremonies (optional — pledge, creed, etc.)
3. Reading and approval of the minutes
   (corrections by unanimous consent; "approved as read/corrected")
4. Reports of officers, boards, standing committees
5. Reports of special (select) committees
6. Special orders (matters set for this meeting, incl. postponed
   items made special orders)
7. Unfinished business and general orders
   (NOT "old business" — improper term; unfinished business is
   never "called for" by the chair asking members, it comes up
   automatically from the previous meeting)
8. New business
9. Announcements
10. Adjournment (by motion, or by unanimous consent, or the chair
    may declare adjournment when the agenda is exhausted)

PART 4 — OFFICERS, RECORDS, COMMITTEES, ELECTIONS

4.1 Officer duties
- PRESIDENT/CHAIR: opens meetings on time, announces business in
  proper order, recognizes members, states and puts all questions,
  enforces the rules, decides points of order (subject to appeal),
  declares adjournment, signs official documents, remains impartial.
- VICE PRESIDENT: presides in the president's absence; automatically
  fills a presidential vacancy (unless bylaws say otherwise).
- SECRETARY: keeps the minutes and official records, maintains the
  membership roll, provides the president an order of business,
  reads documents, handles official correspondence, certifies
  copies. In the absence of president AND VP, the secretary calls
  the meeting to order and presides over election of a chair pro tem.
- TREASURER: custodian of funds, pays authorized bills, reports
  regularly. The treasurer's report is NOT adopted or approved —
  it is received and FILED; only the AUDITOR'S report is adopted.
- PARLIAMENTARIAN: ADVISES the chair on procedure; has no authority
  to rule (only the chair rules); remains impartial, so does not
  make motions, debate, or vote (except by ballot).

4.2 Minutes — the record of what was DONE, not what was said
Contain: kind of meeting (regular/special); name of body; date,
time, place; presence of president and secretary (or substitutes);
whether previous minutes were approved; all MAIN motions (except
withdrawn ones) with the name of the MAKER (not the seconder) and
the result; points of order and appeals; counts on counted votes;
time of adjournment. The secretary signs the minutes.
Do NOT include: opinions, debate summaries, praise or criticism.
Minutes are read and approved at the next regular meeting;
corrections are usually handled by unanimous consent.

4.3 Committees
- STANDING committees: permanent, defined in bylaws (e.g., finance).
- SPECIAL (select/ad hoc) committees: created for one task; cease
  to exist when the task is done and the final report is made.
- Committees may be appointed by the chair, elected, or named in
  the motion to commit.
- Committee chairs usually may debate and vote (small-board rules:
  no seconds required, chair may participate, informal discussion).
- A committee cannot punish members or act beyond its instructions.
- Committee of the whole / quasi-committee / informal consideration:
  ways the full assembly loosens debate limits on a subject.
- Minority of a committee may present its views ("minority report")
  only by permission of the assembly.

4.4 Nominations and elections
- Nomination methods: by the chair, from the floor (most common),
  by a nominating committee, by ballot, by mail, by petition.
- Nominations from the floor require NO second.
- A member may be elected even without being nominated (write-in),
  unless the bylaws require nomination.
- Closing nominations: motion requires 2/3 (it removes rights);
  reopening nominations: majority.
- Elections: MAJORITY of ballots cast elects, unless bylaws provide
  for plurality. If no candidate achieves majority, balloting is
  REPEATED (no automatic runoff or drop-the-lowest unless bylaws
  say so) until someone does.
- An election becomes final when the candidate is declared elected
  and does not decline; an officer assumes office immediately
  unless bylaws set a term start.
- Illegal votes (unintelligible, for ineligible persons) are counted
  as votes CAST for majority computation; blanks are not.

4.5 Improper ("out of order") motions — always out of order:
- Conflicts with the bylaws or with national/state/local law.
- Presents substantially the same question already decided this
  session (renewal must wait for a new session, or use
  reconsider/rescind).
- Conflicts with a motion already adopted and still in force.
- Proposed while another has the floor, or beyond the object of
  the assembly (unless 2/3 allow).
- Dilatory, absurd, or frivolous motions — the chair does not
  entertain them.

PART 5 — FBLA BYLAWS AND ORGANIZATION (the ~10 bylaws questions)

5.1 Structure
- Divisions: FBLA Middle School, FBLA High School, FBLA Collegiate,
  FBLA Network (formerly: FBLA-Middle Level, FBLA, PBL, and
  Professional Division).
- Five national regions (HS/MS): Eastern, Southern, North Central,
  Mountain Plains, Western. International chapters: Eastern Region.
- FBLA High School national officer team (9): president, secretary,
  treasurer, parliamentarian, and five regional vice presidents.
- National officers: elected by voting delegates at the National
  Leadership Conference; serve ONE-YEAR terms; the officer team
  designs and implements the annual program of work.
- Governance: Board of Directors (CEO, business leaders, state
  educators, business education teachers, division national
  presidents).
- Headquarters: Reston, Virginia (National Center opened 1991).

5.2 Membership and chapters
- Membership types: active (student), professional (FBLA Network),
  honorary; local chapters chartered through schools.
- State chapter charter requires at least 5 active local chapters
  (High School division).
- National dues deadline for NLC eligibility: March 1, 11:59 p.m.
  Eastern, of the current school year.
- Members compete: one individual/team event + one chapter event
  at NLC; top-10 NLC finishers may not repeat an event.

5.3 The parliamentarian in FBLA
- Chapter/state/national parliamentarians advise their presidents
  on RONR procedure. The NAP (National Association of
  Parliamentarians) partners with FBLA: objective test questions
  come from the NAP test bank, and top scorers on this test
  historically qualify for NAP membership consideration.

DRILL PRIORITIES (what the test actually hammers)
1. The chart: second/debatable/amendable/vote for all 13 ranked
   motions + incidentals. Expect MANY questions like "Which of
   these motions is not debatable?"
2. Precedence puzzles: "A motion to amend is pending. Which of the
   following is in order?"
3. Two-thirds list: previous question, limit/extend debate, suspend
   the rules, objection to consideration, rescind without notice,
   close nominations.
4. Special rules: tie sustains chair on appeal; reconsider requires
   prevailing side; treasurer's report is filed not adopted;
   minutes record what was done; quorum defaults; "unfinished" not
   "old" business; friendly amendments still need consent.
5. FBLA structure: 9 officers, 5 regions, March 1, NLC voting
   delegates elect officers.
